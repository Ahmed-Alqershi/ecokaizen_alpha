# Template Descriptions for CGE Model Builder

Below are the four template models available in the platform. These descriptions are written to help you choose the one that best fits your use case. Select based on your modeling needs, data availability, and level of economic detail required.

> ⚙️ **Note:** All templates are fully customizable. You can add or remove sectors, production factors, household types, and other structural elements to better match your specific economy or research scenario. The templates serve as starting points, not constraints.

---

## 1. ✨ Simple CGE Model (2 Goods, 2 Factors, 1 Household)

**Best for:** Educational use, small-scale experiments, learning CGE fundamentals

**Key Features:**

* Two goods (Bread, Milk)
* Two production factors (Capital, Labor)
* One representative household
* No government, trade, or investment components
* Pure closed economy with basic Cobb-Douglas production and utility

**Why choose this?**
This model is ideal for beginners or classroom settings. It helps users understand the core building blocks of CGE models—like market clearing, factor allocation, and utility maximization—without any complications. Perfect for small-scale policy shocks and foundational learning.

---

## 2. 📊 Standard CGE Model (Full Domestic Economy with Taxes & Trade)

**Best for:** Policy simulations, national-level planning, intermediate users

**Key Features:**

* Includes taxes, government, savings, investment, and foreign sector
* Models intermediate inputs, factor markets, and production taxes
* Armington function for imports and CET for exports
* Household consumption, government spending, and capital formation all modeled
* Output: Changes in prices, outputs, trade flows, and welfare indicators (e.g., EV)

**Why choose this?**
If you’re looking to analyze real-world policy issues like tariffs, tax changes, or foreign shocks, this is your go-to model. It offers rich structure while staying manageable. It’s perfect for applied research, consulting work, or pilot policy analysis.

---

## 3. 🇨🇲 Cameroon CGE Model (Detailed Sectoral Model for a Developing Economy)

**Best for:** Sectoral studies, development planning, researchers working on African economies

**Key Features:**

* Multi-sector (11 sectors) economy based on Cameroon’s real data
* Labor categories by skill/region (e.g., rural, unskilled urban, skilled urban)
* Captures taxation, government, export-import dynamics, and investment flows
* Suitable for simulations on industrial policy, labor allocation, and trade

**Why choose this?**
This is a country-specific model template inspired by the World Bank framework, perfect for development agencies, ministries, or researchers focusing on economic reform in African or similar economies. Provides detailed disaggregation and realism.

---

## 4. 🇰🇷 Korea CGE Model (3-Sector Economy with Household Types)

**Best for:** Studying economic transformation, industrialization, or equity

**Key Features:**

* Three sectors: agriculture, industry, and services
* Two household types: labor-based and capital-based income
* Includes taxes, government, capital markets, savings, and external borrowing
* Designed around Korea’s development in the 1960s
* Tracks sectoral income distribution, capital-labor balance, and welfare

**Why choose this?**
If you're exploring themes like income inequality, structural change, or trade-driven development, this model offers a compact yet rich structure. It’s historically based but flexible enough for comparative or educational use.

---

Let your modeling goals guide your choice. If you're not sure where to start, we recommend beginning with the Simple CGE Model and gradually exploring more detailed templates as your needs evolve.

> 🔧 **All templates are editable.** You can modify the number of goods, sectors, institutions, or behaviors to suit your analysis. You’re never locked into the structure of a chosen model.
